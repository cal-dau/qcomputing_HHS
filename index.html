01 — Foundation

What is Quantum
Computing?

Classical computers store data as bits — binary switches that are either 0 or 1. Quantum computers use qubits, which exploit quantum mechanical phenomena to represent and process information in fundamentally new ways.

Where a classical computer evaluates one path at a time, a quantum computer can explore many computational paths simultaneously, making certain classes of problems tractable that would otherwise require astronomical computation time.

The key insight: nature at its smallest scales follows rules so different from our everyday experience that they enable an entirely new kind of computing machine — one that thinks in probabilities, interference, and entanglement.

Quantum computers are not simply faster classical computers. They excel at specific problem types — optimization, simulation, cryptography — while classical machines remain superior for most everyday tasks.

02 — Key Concepts

The Building
Blocks

Four foundational phenomena make quantum computing possible. Understanding these unlocks the logic behind the machines.

⚛️
Core Principle

Superposition

A qubit can exist as 0, 1, or any combination of both simultaneously until measured. This is not merely metaphorical — the qubit physically occupies multiple states at once, allowing a quantum processor with n qubits to represent 2ⁿ states in parallel.

Think of it like a coin spinning in the air: it is neither heads nor tails until it lands. Quantum computation happens while the coin is still spinning.

🔗
Quantum Correlation

Entanglement

Two qubits can become entangled — their quantum states linked regardless of the physical distance between them. Measuring one instantly determines the state of the other, a phenomenon Einstein famously called "spooky action at a distance."

Entanglement allows quantum computers to coordinate information across qubits in ways classical systems fundamentally cannot replicate, exponentially increasing computational power.

〰️
Wave Mechanics

Interference

Like waves in water, quantum probability amplitudes can add together or cancel each other out. Quantum algorithms are carefully designed to amplify the probability of correct answers and suppress wrong ones through constructive and destructive interference.

Without interference, a quantum computer's output would be random noise. It is the precision engineering of interference that turns superposition into useful computation.

📦
Measurement Theory

Decoherence & Measurement

When a qubit is measured or interacts with its environment, its quantum state collapses into a definite classical value. Decoherence — unwanted environmental interactions — is the primary engineering challenge of quantum computing.

Current quantum processors operate near absolute zero (−273°C) and are shielded from vibration, radiation, and electromagnetic noise to preserve quantum states long enough to complete computations.

03 — Real-World Impact

Where Quantum
Changes Everything

Quantum advantage is emerging across industries where classical computation reaches its limits. These are the fields where the impact will first be felt.

01

Drug Discovery & Medicine

Simulating molecular interactions at quantum scale to design new drugs and materials. What takes years on classical supercomputers could take hours — potentially revolutionizing treatment for diseases like Alzheimer's and cancer.

02

Cryptography & Security

Quantum computers will eventually break RSA encryption. Quantum key distribution (QKD) offers theoretically unbreakable encryption — any eavesdropping disturbs the quantum state, making interception detectable by the laws of physics.

03

Climate & Energy

Optimizing electrical grids, modeling climate systems, and designing next-generation batteries and solar cells. Quantum simulation of the nitrogen fixation process could unlock room-temperature superconductors and reduce fertilizer energy costs by 2%.

04

Financial Modeling

Portfolio optimization, risk analysis, and fraud detection across billions of transactions. Quantum algorithms like Monte Carlo can price complex derivatives and model market scenarios exponentially faster than today's methods.

05

Artificial Intelligence

Quantum machine learning algorithms promise to speed up training and inference on massive datasets. Quantum neural networks may unlock pattern recognition capabilities far beyond current AI, particularly in unstructured data analysis.

06

Logistics & Supply Chain

The "traveling salesman" class of optimization problems — routing, scheduling, resource allocation — grows exponentially hard for classical computers. Quantum annealing and QAOA algorithms tackle these directly, saving billions annually.

04 — Local Connections

Quantum in Alabama

Alabama is not on the sidelines of the quantum revolution. Research institutions, federal agencies, and defense contractors across the state are deeply embedded in the quantum computing ecosystem.

University of Alabama in Huntsville (UAH)

UAH's physics and engineering departments conduct research in quantum optics, quantum sensing, and quantum communication. The university partners with nearby federal agencies on emerging quantum technologies.

NASA Marshall Space Flight Center

Located in Huntsville, Marshall explores quantum computing for aerospace applications including trajectory optimization, propulsion simulation, and next-generation sensor development for deep space missions.

Redstone Arsenal & DEVCOM

The Army's Combat Capabilities Development Command at Redstone Arsenal investigates quantum sensing and quantum-resistant cryptography for national defense applications and secure military communications.

University of Alabama (Tuscaloosa)

UA researchers work in quantum materials and condensed matter physics, contributing to the theoretical foundations of topological qubits and novel quantum hardware architectures.

Auburn University

Auburn's Samuel Ginn College of Engineering includes research in quantum information science, quantum error correction, and quantum photonics, preparing the next generation of quantum engineers.

Alabama Supercomputer Authority

The ASA provides high-performance computing resources to Alabama universities and industry, and is actively evaluating quantum-classical hybrid workflows to extend its capabilities for researchers statewide.
